Motivation

Is a PhD still worth it?

The PhD is an investment in a specific stock of human capital: reading, modeling, estimating, writing, refereeing. The return to that capital depends on how much of it AI now supplies at or below its cost.

Two scenarios stress-test the question. In the first, AI does all of it at human level or above. In the second, AI does some of it, and the rest is augmented. The talk walks through both and asks what survives.

Scenario A

Full automation

Assume AI performs every task in the research pipeline at human level or above — including idea generation.

Tasks performed by AI
  • Idea generation
  • Reading and synthesizing literature
  • Theory and mathematical modeling
  • Identification and research design
  • Data collection and cleaning
  • Estimation and inference
  • Writing and revision
  • Refereeing
What is left for humans

Evaluation of final output

Judging whether the answer is correct, credible, and relevant.

Accountability for policy prescriptions

Institutions and professions assign responsibility to people, not systems.

Implication. The premium on PhD-specific human capital falls sharply. But operating in this world requires fluency with the tools. Learning them is a prerequisite, not an option.
3 / 19
Scenario B

Augmentation

AI substitutes for some tasks, complements others. The productivity gain is captured by those who know how to use the tools.

Substitution

Routine tasks

Data cleaning, code, reference formatting, first drafts.

Complementarity

Judgment tasks

Identification strategy, interpretation, field-specific intuition, choosing what matters.

Residual premium

Taste and agenda

Picking questions that are worth answering; knowing when a result is important.

Economic logic. Task-based framework (Autor, Acemoglu, etc.): AI shifts which tasks machines can do; gains flow to workers whose skills complement the remaining tasks, and to those who adopt the tools early.

Under both scenarios, learning the tools dominates not learning them. The rest of this talk maps which research tasks are most affected and how to work with agents today.

Background

What an AI agent is

An AI agent is software with an LLM at its core. The software gathers input, enforces rules, mediates tool calls, and runs the loop — without a human directing each step.
Step 1

Perceive

Software assembles context — files, data, prior state, user prompt.

Step 2

Reason

LLM outputs text — either an answer, or a structured tool call specifying what to do next.

Step 3

Act

Software routes the LLM's tool calls — code, search, APIs, file edits.

Step 4

Loop

Software feeds the result back and continues until the goal is met.

You shape the software layer. Custom instructions, hooks, skills, house rules — these live in the software around the LLM. That is where you guide the agent's behavior.

2023

Tool use

2024–26

Reasoning and coding agents

Step-by-step reasoning; agents that write, run, and debug full codebases.

Agents are useful now because three capabilities converged: a strong general-purpose model, reliable tool calling, and long-running autonomous loops.

Deep dive · Reading

Literature and synthesis

What agents do well
  • Map a field from a handful of seed papers.
  • Extract arguments, assumptions, data, methods.
  • Summarize a full paper with citations to sections.
  • Surface related work and identify gaps.
Where they fail
  • Fabricated citations and DOI errors.
  • Shallow synthesis when the prompt is shallow.
  • Missing the paper's contribution, not just its content.
Rule. Always verify cites against the source. Treat synthesis as a draft you edit, not a reference you cite.

Deep dive · Empirics

Coding and empirics

The highest-leverage area today. Cost per line of correct code has collapsed.

What agents do well
  • Translate between Stata, R, Python, Julia.
  • Write regressions, tables, plots.
  • Debug stack traces and failing tests.
  • Clean messy datasets and reshape panels.
  • Run existing replication packages.
What you still own
  • Specification choices and their economic meaning.
  • Identification strategy and its assumptions.
  • Sanity checking: do the numbers make sense?
  • Deciding what to drop and what to keep.
Use agents to move faster on tasks you already know how to evaluate. Do not use them to generate estimates you cannot check.
10 / 19
Deep dive · Writing

Writing and revision

What agents do well
  • Tighten prose; cut redundancy; enforce style.
  • Restructure an introduction around a claim.
  • Draft referee responses from comments plus your notes.
  • Translate between formats: slides ↔ paper ↔ abstract.
What you still own
  • The argument and its framing.
  • Claims made — and their strength.
  • The voice of the paper.
Write the first draft of your ideas yourself. Use the model to revise, not to originate.

Deep dive · Design

Research design and taste

Where agents are weakest today — and where PhD-specific value holds longest.

Weak today

Picking questions that matter

Models can brainstorm hundreds of questions. Choosing the one worth a two-year investment requires field context they do not yet carry.

Weak today

Identification that holds up

Models recognize textbook designs. They are weaker at spotting novel sources of variation or at defending assumptions under scrutiny.

Still yours

Taste

Which result is interesting; which robustness check is the real test; what the profession will find convincing.

Still yours

Agenda

Connecting papers into a coherent research program over years.

How to work with models

The working loop

Specify the goal and the constraints. Let the model plan. Have sub-agents audit and test independently. Evaluate against the goal.

01

Prompt the goal

State what "done" looks like. Broad, not step-by-step.

02

Guide behavior

Context, hooks, skills, custom instructions, house rules.

03

Model plans & executes

Ask it to propose an approach, then carry it out.

04

Sub-agents verify

Autonomous checks for compliance and output validation.

05

You evaluate

Final output against the original goal. This is your job.

Posture. Do not micromanage steps. Specify goal + constraints + verification. If you cannot define verification, you cannot delegate the task.

How to work with models

Extending the models

The base model is the substrate. Your setup is what makes it yours.

Your tools

Custom capabilities

Scripts, APIs, database queries, lab-specific utilities exposed to the model. The model gains whatever the tool can do.

Hooks

Automated triggers

Actions that fire before or after the model acts — formatting, linting, logging, blocking unsafe commands.

Skills

Reusable instructions

Packaged know-how the model loads on demand. A standardized way to encode your workflow.

Sub-agents

Verification and specialization

Delegate checks, reviews, or focused sub-tasks to isolated agents whose output the main agent consumes.

The pattern: start with the built-in capabilities, identify what your workflow needs that is missing, add it as a tool or skill, test, repeat.

How to work with models

Infrastructure around the model

Versioning

Git — for everything

Code, prompts, instructions, notes. Every change reversible; every session reproducible.

Plans

Markdown files

Work plans, task lists, house rules — written in .md the model can read and update in place.

Knowledge

Obsidian (or similar)

A persistent knowledge base for papers, ideas, and project notes that the model can search.

Model-agnostic. The loop and the infrastructure are the same across Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and what comes next. Invest in the workflow — model choice is a switch, not a rewrite.

Calibration

Where agents fail

Hallucination

Fabricated citations

Confidently wrong references. Always check DOI, authors, year, venue.

Math

Confident wrong derivations

Step-by-step reasoning helps but does not solve it. Verify non-trivial algebra.

Code

Over-eager refactors

Rewrites more than you asked; silently changes behavior. Read diffs.

Knowledge

Stale world model

Cutoff dates miss recent papers, APIs, syntax. Provide current context.

Judgment

Weak field-specific taste

Does not know which specification is standard in your subfield.

Scale

Context limits

Long tasks drift. Break work into verifiable chunks with checkpoints.

Practice

Strategy for PhD students

Do

Learn to operate agents

Prompting, context, verification, tool setup. This is the new baseline literacy.

Do

Move faster on evaluable tasks

If you can check the output in minutes, delegate the first pass.

Don't

Outsource training

Do not skip the parts of the PhD you are there to learn — identification, derivations, first drafts.

Don't

Publish what you can't verify

If you cannot independently check a result, you cannot put your name on it.

Rule of thumb. Use agents where you already have taste. Build taste on everything else.

Norms

Attribution and reproducibility

The discipline's standards for transparency extend to AI-assisted work. Documentation is the mechanism.

Attribution
  • Disclose substantive AI use in the paper (method, scope, role).
  • Follow journal and funder guidance; norms are tightening.
  • AI is not a co-author. Responsibility remains with you.
Reproducibility
  • Version prompts, instructions, and configuration alongside code.
  • Log model, version, date for any agent-run step that affects results.
  • Treat AI-produced code and data like any other artifact: archive, cite, release.
Documentation is key. If a reviewer — or future you — cannot reconstruct what the model did, the result is not reproducible.
